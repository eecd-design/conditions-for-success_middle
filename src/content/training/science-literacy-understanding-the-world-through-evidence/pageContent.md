## Course Overview

In an age of rapid technological change and information overload, the ability to evaluate scientific claims has never been more essential. This course introduces learners to the core principles of science literacy: how scientific knowledge is built, tested, and revised over time. Rather than focusing on memorizing facts from any single discipline, the course emphasizes the underlying logic of scientific reasoning — how hypotheses are formed, how evidence is gathered, and how conclusions are drawn responsibly. Students will leave with practical tools for distinguishing well-supported claims from speculation, marketing, or misinformation, whether they encounter them in the news, on social media, or in everyday conversation.

## What You Will Learn

This course is organized around four key competencies that form the foundation of scientific thinking. By the end of the course, students will be able to:

* Identify the difference between correlation and causation in real-world data
* Evaluate the quality and reliability of a scientific source or study
* Recognize common statistical and rhetorical pitfalls used to mislead audiences
* Apply basic principles of the scientific method to everyday questions and decisions

Each module builds on the last, moving from foundational concepts toward more applied, real-world case studies.

## Course Structure and Topics

The course unfolds across six weekly modules, each combining short readings, video lectures, and discussion-based exercises. Early modules establish the basics of the scientific method, including hypothesis formation, experimental design, and the role of peer review. Middle modules shift toward data interpretation, covering topics such as sample size, statistical significance, and the dangers of cherry-picked evidence. The final modules apply these skills to contemporary issues, including public health debates, climate science, and the spread of misinformation online. Throughout, students engage directly with real studies and news articles rather than abstract examples, reinforcing how these concepts apply outside the classroom.

## Who Should Take This Course

This course is designed for a broad audience and assumes no prior background in science or statistics. It is well suited for:

* Students preparing for further study in science, journalism, or public policy
* Professionals who regularly interpret data or research in their work
* Curious learners who want to better evaluate health, environmental, or technology news
* Educators looking to strengthen their own critical thinking before teaching it to others

No specialized equipment or software is required; all that's needed is a willingness to question claims and examine evidence carefully.

## Assessment and Certification

Progress is measured through a mix of low-stakes formative activities and a final applied project. Weekly quizzes reinforce key vocabulary and concepts, while short written reflections ask students to analyze a real scientific claim using the tools introduced that week. The course culminates in a capstone project, in which students select a current scientific or health-related controversy and produce a short report evaluating the strength of the evidence on each side. Students who complete all modules and the capstone project with a passing grade will receive a certificate of completion, which can be added to a resume or professional learning portfolio.
