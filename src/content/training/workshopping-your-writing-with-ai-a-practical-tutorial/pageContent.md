## Introduction

Writing has always benefited from a second pair of eyes — an editor, a writing partner, a workshop group willing to ask hard questions about a draft. AI tools can now play a version of that role, available any time and at any stage of the process. This tutorial is designed to help writers use AI thoughtfully as a workshopping partner, not a replacement for their own voice or judgment. Rather than asking AI to simply "fix" or "write" a piece, the goal is to use it the way you'd use a trusted reader: to surface blind spots, test ideas, and sharpen what is already there. The techniques below apply equally well to essays, fiction, professional writing, and academic work.

## Getting Started: Setting Up a Productive Session

Before diving into specific techniques, it helps to frame the AI's role clearly at the start of a session. Tell it what kind of feedback you want — developmental notes on structure and argument, line-level feedback on style, or a gut-check on tone — rather than leaving the task open-ended. This section covers the groundwork for an effective workshop session:

* Share the piece in full, along with its intended audience and purpose
* Specify what stage of revision you're in (early draft vs. polishing)
* Ask for questions before asking for solutions, to avoid premature rewrites
* Set boundaries on how much the AI should rewrite versus simply flag

Establishing this context up front prevents generic feedback and keeps the AI focused on what actually matters for your draft.

## Core Techniques for AI-Assisted Workshopping

Once a session is set up, several techniques tend to produce the most useful feedback. Asking the AI to summarize your piece back to you in its own words is a fast way to check whether your main point is actually landing as intended; a mismatch between your intent and the summary often reveals an structural issue worth addressing. Asking it to play devil's advocate against your central argument can expose weak reasoning or unaddressed counterpoints before a real reader finds them. For style and voice work, asking the AI to identify patterns — overused words, repetitive sentence structures, inconsistent tone — tends to be more useful than asking it to rewrite passages outright, since the latter risks flattening your voice into something more generic.

## Common Pitfalls to Avoid

AI feedback is most useful when it's treated as one input among many, not a final verdict. A few pitfalls are worth watching for as you incorporate this tool into your process:

* Accepting suggested rewrites wholesale without checking that they still sound like you
* Asking for feedback before you've formed your own opinion of the draft's weaknesses
* Treating AI praise as a reliable signal that a piece is finished
* Using AI to skip the discomfort of getting feedback from real human readers

The goal is to use AI to think more clearly about your own writing, not to outsource the thinking itself.

## Bringing It Into Your Regular Practice

The most effective way to integrate AI into a writing practice is to treat each session as a structured conversation rather than a one-off request. Start with a clear question, work through a few rounds of follow-up based on what the AI surfaces, and end each session by writing a short note to yourself about what you actually plan to change and why. Over time, this builds a habit of using AI feedback as a starting point for your own critical judgment, rather than as an external authority on what "good" writing looks like. Writers who use this iterative approach tend to retain their voice while still benefiting from a sharper, more consistent editorial eye throughout the drafting process.
